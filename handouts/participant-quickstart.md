# Participant Quickstart

## The Five-Step Codex Habit

1. Give the goal.
2. Point Codex to the evidence.
3. State the privacy boundary.
4. Ask for verification.
5. Ask for a handoff when the work should be reusable.

## Copy/Paste Starter

```text
You are working in this local folder. First inspect the relevant files and docs.
Goal: [what I need produced].
Audience: [who will use it].
Source boundary: use [local files/repo docs/scripts] first.
Privacy boundary: keep the output [aggregate-only/sanitized/internal].
Verification: run or describe [checks/tests/scripts/manual review].
Close out with what you used, what changed, what was verified, and what remains uncertain.
```

## What To Include In A Good Request

- Folder, file, or repo name.
- Intended output: table, memo, report, script, dashboard, review, or handoff.
- Audience and level of detail.
- What to exclude.
- Whether identifiers may appear.
- How current the answer needs to be.
- What counts as done.

## Good First Tasks

- Summarize a local CSV or spreadsheet into an aggregate table.
- Compare two snapshots and explain what changed.
- Separate renewal or action items from planning adjustments.
- Check the actual status of a workflow or job.
- Turn notes into a sanitized handoff or lessons-learned memo.
- Review a small code or documentation change for risks.
- Create training examples, synthetic data, and an answer key.
- Improve a training repo by adding examples, checking links, and writing release notes.

## What To Watch For

- Generic summaries that do not cite the local evidence.
- Polished reports that include private source details.
- Answers that treat ambiguous identity or ownership as certain.
- Totals that mix renewals, adjustments, and non-software costs.
- Status reports that infer success without checking the actual workflow.

## Ask Codex To Slow Down When Needed

```text
Before answering, tell me which files, columns, or scripts you are relying on.
If the evidence is missing or ambiguous, say that explicitly.
```
